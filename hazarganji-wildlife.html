Animal Wildlife

Markhor: The park is known for hosting a significant population of the rare and endangered Balochistan
markhor, a wild goat species with distinct spiral horns.

Leopard: The elusive and majestic Persian leopard is found in the rocky terrains
of the park, along with other smaller feline species.

Chinkara: Also known as the Indian Gazelle, these graceful antelopes
are commonly spotted in the park's open plains.

Caracal: A medium-sized wild cat with distinctive tufted ears,
the caracal can be found in the park's scrublands.

Chukar Partridge: Various bird species, including the Chukar partridge (Alectoris chukar), inhabit the park.

Urial Sheep: Another important species in the park is the Urial sheep (Ovis vignei), a wild sheep species adapted to the rocky terrains.

Flora: Juniper Forests: The park features juniper forests, particularly in the higher
elevations, contributing to the unique plant diversity. Tamarisk: Found along riverbanks, tamarisk trees are crucial for preventing soil erosion and
maintaining the ecological balance.

Urial Sheep

The urial also known as arkars, shapo, or shapu, is a wild sheep native to Central and South Asia. It is listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List. Urial.

The urial inhabits western central Asia from the northeast of Iran and the west of Kazakhstan to Balochistan in Pakistan and the Ladakh regions in North India. It lives in steep grassy terrain below the tree line. It may also occur in agricultural fields and occasionally in partly wooded, mountainous areas.

Leopard

Leopards are the smallest of the large cats, which includes lions, tigers, and jaguars. Female leopards weigh 46 to 132 pounds and males weigh 80 to 165 pounds. They average 28 inches at the shoulder with a general range of 17.5 to 30.5 inches high at the shoulder.

The common leopards are the largest predators of its ecosystem and they play an important role in maintaining its health. They keep a control on the population of herbivores and remove the unhealthy animals from its habitat.

Chukar Partridge

The chukar is a rotund 32–35 cm (13–14 in) long partridge, with a light brown back, grey breast, and buff belly. The shades vary across the various populations.

The chukar partridge was declared the national bird in 1985 due to its historical and cultural significance in Pakistan. This bird has been a part of the region's ecosystem for centuries and has become intertwined with the country's heritage.

Markhor

Markhors are the largest of the wild goat species. They have broad hooves and striking spiral horns that can grow to 5 feet long in mature males; female horns are smaller, around 2 feet. The markhor's coat is reddish gray with a dark stripe running the length of its back.

As a mammal, markhor mothers give birth to live young. After a gestation period of 135-170 days, they give birth to 1-2 kids. The kids are weaned at 5-6 months. Markhor can be expected to live around 10-13 years in the wild and up to 19 years in captivity.

Hazarganji National Park's diverse ecosystems make it a vital area for conservation, and efforts are ongoing to protect and preserve the rich biodiversity that thrives within its boundaries. Visitors to the park can experience the unique blend of wildlife, stunning landscapes, and cultural heritage that makes Hingol a remarkable destination for nature enthusiasts.
